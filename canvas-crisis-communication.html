When Silence Becomes the Message

The Canvas security incident showed me that crisis communication is not only about getting the facts right. It is also about showing people that the organization is present while those facts are still developing.

By Tobias Khabie · IMC 306

In May 2026, Canvas was disrupted by a cyberattack that affected schools and universities across the country during finals season. For some students, the timing created immediate academic consequences: delayed projects, rescheduled exams, and uncertainty at the exact moment when reliability mattered most. At Northwestern, the impact felt more limited, but as a student who uses Canvas and studies strategic communication, I was interested in how Instructure would respond.

The situation became a useful case study because it involved two competing crisis priorities. On one hand, organizations need to avoid speaking too quickly before they understand what happened. In a cybersecurity incident, inaccurate communication can create legal, technical, and reputational problems. On the other hand, silence can create its own damage. When students, faculty, and institutions are waiting for answers, a lack of consistent updates can make people feel forgotten, even if work is happening behind the scenes.

Fact-finding cannot replace presence

Instructure CEO Steve Daly later acknowledged that the company focused too much on fact-finding and did not communicate consistently enough while users were waiting for information. That admission stood out to me because it sounded like something we would workshop in class. The instinct to verify information was not wrong. The problem was the balance. In crisis communication, audiences do not expect every answer immediately, but they do expect evidence that the organization understands the urgency of the moment.

That is where holding statements become valuable. A strong holding statement does not need to solve the entire crisis. It needs to acknowledge the issue, identify who is affected, explain what is being done, and set expectations for future updates. Even a short update can reduce uncertainty if it is honest, specific, and repeated on a clear timeline.

Trust is built between updates

The Canvas incident also reminded me that reputation is shaped during the gaps between major announcements. If a company waits until it has a complete answer, users may fill the silence with frustration, speculation, or distrust. This is especially true for a platform like Canvas, which sits at the center of academic work. When the platform goes down during finals, the issue is not abstract. It affects grades, schedules, faculty plans, and students' sense of control.

From a strategic communications perspective, the lesson is clear: speed and accuracy should not be treated as opposites. The goal is not to rush unverified claims into the public. The goal is to communicate what is known, what is not known, what is being investigated, and when people can expect the next update.

My takeaway

This case helped me understand why crisis communication requires a plan before the crisis happens. Organizations need decision rules, approval processes, spokespersons, and update schedules ready before they are under pressure. Without that structure, even well-intended caution can look like avoidance.

The Canvas breach was a reminder that communication is part of the response itself. In a crisis, silence does not feel neutral to the people affected. It becomes a message. The challenge for communicators is to make sure that message does not become, “We are not here when you need us.”
