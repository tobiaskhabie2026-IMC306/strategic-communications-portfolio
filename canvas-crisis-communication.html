When Silence Becomes the Message

The Canvas security incident showed me that crisis communication is not only about getting the facts right. It is also about showing people that the organization is present while those facts are still developing.

By Tobias Khabie · IMC 306

When Canvas was disrupted by a cyberattack during finals season, the communication response became just as important as the technical response. Some students faced delayed exams, postponed projects, and uncertainty at exactly the moment when they needed reliable information.

As a student and strategic communications learner, I was interested in how Instructure would balance accuracy with urgency. In a cybersecurity incident, speaking too quickly can create problems. But staying quiet for too long can make affected users feel ignored.

Fact-finding cannot replace presence

Instructure CEO Steve Daly later acknowledged that the company focused too much on fact-finding and did not communicate consistently enough while users were waiting. That admission stood out because the instinct to verify information was not wrong. The issue was balance.

A holding statement does not need to solve the entire crisis. It needs to acknowledge the issue, identify who is affected, explain what is being done, and set expectations for the next update. Even a brief message can reduce uncertainty if it is honest and repeated consistently.

Trust is built between updates

The Canvas incident reminded me that reputation is shaped in the space between major announcements. If an organization waits until it has a complete answer, users may fill the silence with frustration or distrust. This is especially true for a platform that sits at the center of academic work.

My takeaway is that speed and accuracy should not be treated as opposites. Communicators can say what is known, what is still being investigated, and when people can expect another update. In a crisis, silence does not feel neutral to the people affected. It becomes a message.
