Data Is Only Valuable When It Shapes Strategy

Numbers become useful when communicators can interpret what they mean, connect them to an objective, and turn them into informed action.

By Tobias Khabie · IMC 306

Our world runs on numbers. Computers process digits, our days are organized by numerical markers, and organizations increasingly depend on data to make decisions. In the digital age, data is often described as the new gold. But not all numbers carry the same value, and merely collecting information does not make an organization strategic.

The value of data lies in the story it reveals. When interpreted properly, it can help leaders understand their audiences, identify priorities, test assumptions, and choose where to focus their resources. Data without interpretation, however, is little more than fool's gold: impressive in volume, but limited in practical value.

Finding meaning behind the numbers

My coursework in data science and strategic communications has taught me to approach data from two complementary perspectives. Data science provides the tools to organize information, recognize patterns, and assess evidence. Strategic communication asks a different set of questions: Why does this pattern matter? What does it tell us about an audience? How should it change the decisions we make?

These perspectives came together during the Smarter Hawkins campaign sprint, a fictional mayoral election project. My role was to analyze polling information about our candidate, the electorate, and the issues that mattered most to voters. The assignment required more than reporting which numbers were highest. I had to determine what those results suggested about the campaign's strongest path forward.

From polling results to campaign choices

The analysis showed that safety and transparency were central audience concerns. Voters wanted to know what was happening in Hawkins, whether their families were safe, and how the next mayor would prepare the community for future threats. Those findings helped our team move from a broad campaign concept to a focused communication strategy.

We built recommendations around clear public updates, practical safety infrastructure, emergency preparedness, and data-informed leadership. We also adapted the message for different audience segments. Parents needed reassurance about family safety, younger voters wanted greater transparency, and business owners needed confidence in the town's stability. The data did not write the campaign for us. It gave us a stronger foundation for deciding what to say, who needed to hear it, and which actions would make the message credible.

Analysis must serve an objective

Data can be enormously valuable in communication settings, but that value is unlocked only through sound analysis, strong data literacy, and a goal-oriented approach. Communicators must be able to recognize which findings are relevant, explain them in accessible language, and connect them to decisions.

My biggest takeaway is that data should never be included simply to make a strategy appear more authoritative. It should challenge assumptions and improve the final recommendation. Numbers become strategically meaningful when they help an organization understand people more clearly and act with greater purpose.
