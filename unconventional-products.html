Selling the Unexpected: What Gravy Ice Cream Taught Me About Positioning

An unconventional product does not need a conventional campaign. It does, however, need a familiar reason for people to care.

By Tobias Khabie · IMC 306

Selling water in a crowded beverage market should be straightforward. Liquid Death made it surprising. By packaging water like an energy drink and building a loud, irreverent identity around an ordinary product, the brand demonstrated that differentiation can be more powerful than the product category itself. Its success helped me understand an important strategic communications lesson: when a product seems unusual, the story surrounding it becomes part of the value.

I applied that lesson during a group campaign sprint for Biscuit Bliss, a fictional brand selling biscuits-and-gravy ice cream sandwiches. The product naturally invited skepticism. Some people might see it as a gimmick, react with disgust, or accuse the brand of turning a traditional Southern meal into a publicity stunt. Our challenge was not to pretend those reactions would not happen. It was to give the product a position strong enough to withstand them.

Make the unfamiliar feel emotionally familiar

Rather than focusing only on novelty, our campaign connected Biscuit Bliss to Southern comfort, hospitality, and nostalgia. We targeted younger Southerners, including people who had moved away from home, and framed the product as a playful way to reconnect with familiar flavors and cultural pride. The message was not simply, “Try this strange ice cream.” It was, “Here is a new expression of something that already feels like home.”

That distinction matters. Novelty can earn attention, but attention alone does not create loyalty. An emotional connection gives audiences a reason to move beyond their first reaction. Our line, “Southern Comfort You Can Hold in Your Hand,” gave the unfamiliar product a recognizable emotional foundation.

Let curiosity do some of the work

Our launch strategy treated surprise as an advantage. We proposed starting with food reviewers, bloggers, and social media creators who could document authentic first reactions. Retro diner-themed pop-ups would then give people a low-risk, social way to taste the product before a wider retail launch. This phased approach turned uncertainty into participation: audiences could watch, discuss, and eventually experience the product themselves.

We also planned for criticism instead of allowing it to define the brand. Responses such as “Don’t judge the biscuit before the first bite” maintained the campaign’s humor while reinforcing confidence in the product. The goal was not to win over everyone. It was to remain consistent, culturally grounded, and memorable for the audience most likely to embrace the idea.

Unconventional still requires fundamentals

The sprint showed me that unusual products do not eliminate the need for communication fundamentals. They make those fundamentals more important. A campaign still needs a defined audience, a relevant insight, a distinct position, coordinated channels, and a plan for reputational risk.

Liquid Death transformed ordinary water through a distinctive brand story. Biscuit Bliss challenged my team to make an extraordinary product feel emotionally understandable. Both examples demonstrate that strategic communication is not about making everyone like an idea. It is about giving the right audience a clear and compelling reason to care.
