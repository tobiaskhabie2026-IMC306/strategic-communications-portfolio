Selling the Unexpected: What Gravy Ice Cream Taught Me About Positioning

An unconventional product does not need a conventional campaign. It does, however, need a familiar reason for people to care.

By Tobias Khabie · IMC 306

Liquid Death showed me that even a familiar product can feel new when the positioning is strong enough. By turning water into something loud, strange, and memorable, the brand proved that the story around a product can become part of the value itself.

I applied that lesson during a group campaign sprint for Biscuit Bliss, a fictional brand selling biscuits-and-gravy ice cream sandwiches. The product was intentionally unconventional, so our challenge was not to hide that fact. Our job was to make the idea feel grounded enough for people to give it a chance.

Make the unfamiliar feel emotionally familiar

Instead of relying only on shock value, our campaign connected Biscuit Bliss to Southern comfort, hospitality, and nostalgia. We positioned the product for younger Southerners, especially people who might see the flavor as a playful connection to home.

Our line, “Southern Comfort You Can Hold in Your Hand,” gave the product a familiar emotional frame. The takeaway for me was simple: novelty earns attention, but meaning gives people a reason to move past their first reaction.

Let curiosity do some of the work

We also treated surprise as an advantage. Food reviewers, bloggers, social creators, and retro diner-style pop-ups could turn skepticism into a shared experience. People might not immediately want gravy ice cream, but they may want to watch someone try it, talk about it, and eventually try it themselves.

This sprint reminded me that unconventional products still require a defined audience, a clear insight, a strong position, and a plan for criticism. Strategic communication is not about making everyone like an idea. It is about giving the right audience a reason to care.
